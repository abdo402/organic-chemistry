// Deep Learning

What are Hydrocarbons?

Hydrocarbons are organic compounds consisting entirely of Carbon (C) and Hydrogen (H). They form the structural backbone of fossil fuels, polymers, and countless synthetic materials.

⬡ Alkanes — Saturated

Alkanes contain the maximum possible hydrogen atoms per carbon. With no multiple bonds, they are chemically stable and relatively unreactive — except in combustion and halogenation reactions. They form a homologous series with the general formula CnH2n+2.

⬡ Alkenes & Alkynes — Unsaturated

Unsaturated hydrocarbons contain double or triple C–C bonds. These π-electron-rich regions are highly reactive, readily undergoing addition reactions with H₂, Br₂, HX, and H₂O — which is why they are critical in industrial syntheses.

⬡ The n=1 Exception

Both alkenes and alkynes cannot exist with a single carbon. A double or triple bond requires at least two carbon atoms as its endpoints. Therefore, Ethene (C₂H₄) and Ethyne (C₂H₂) are the minimum members of their respective series.

// Naming Science

IUPAC Nomenclature

The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry rules ensure every molecule has one unique, unambiguous systematic name. Follow these five steps in order.

Find the Parent Chain

Identify the longest continuous chain of carbon atoms. This determines the root name: Meth- (1C), Eth- (2C), Prop- (3C), But- (4C), Pent- (5C), Hex- (6C), Hept- (7C), Oct- (8C), Non- (9C), Dec- (10C).

Key rule for unsaturated compounds: The parent chain must include the double or triple bond — even if a longer chain exists that excludes it.

ExampleCH₃–CH₂–CH=CH₂ → Parent chain = 4 carbons → "But-"

The Lowest Locant Rule

Number the chain from the end that gives the lowest possible locant to the first point of difference — multiple bonds have priority over simple alkyl substituents.

  • Start numbering from the end closest to the double/triple bond.
  • If no multiple bonds: number from the end nearest the first branch.
  • If tied: use alphabetical order to break the tie.

Name the Substituents

Branches not part of the parent chain are named by replacing "-ane" with "-yl":

  • –CH₃ → Methyl
  • –C₂H₅ → Ethyl
  • –C₃H₇ → Propyl

Use prefixes di-, tri-, tetra- for identical groups: e.g., 2,2-dimethyl...

Alphabetical Priority

When multiple different substituents are present, list them in strict alphabetical order — based on the substituent name itself, ignoring multiplicative prefixes (di, tri, etc.).

Correct orderEthyl comes before Methyl → "...ethyl...methyl..."

Final Construction

Assemble the full name as: [locant]-[substituent]-[parent][suffix]

  • Use commas between numbers: 2,3-
  • Use hyphens between numbers and words: 2-methyl
  • No space before the parent chain name.
Full worked example2,2-dimethylpentane  ·  3-ethyl-2-methylhexane
// Structure Logic

Isomerism: Same Formula, Different Structure

Isomers share the same molecular formula but differ in structural arrangement or spatial orientation — often resulting in dramatically different physical and chemical properties.

Type I

Structural (Constitutional) Isomers

Atoms bonded in a different order or connectivity. Three sub-types:

  • Chain isomers: Different skeleton branching (e.g., n-Butane vs Isobutane).
  • Position isomers: Same functional group, different location (e.g., 1-Butene vs 2-Butene).
  • Functional group isomers: Different functional groups (e.g., Ethanol vs Dimethyl Ether — both C₂H₆O).
Type II

Stereoisomers (3D Arrangement)

Same bonding connectivity, but different spatial arrangement:

  • Geometric (Cis/Trans): Restricted rotation around a C=C double bond produces two distinct 3D arrangements.
  • Optical (Enantiomers): Non-superimposable mirror images arising from a chiral carbon centre. Critical in pharmaceutical chemistry.
