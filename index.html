// Deep Learning

What are Hydrocarbons?

Hydrocarbons are organic compounds consisting entirely of Carbon (C) and Hydrogen (H). They form the structural backbone of fossil fuels, polymers, and countless synthetic materials.

⬡ Alkanes — Saturated

Alkanes contain the maximum possible hydrogen atoms per carbon. With no multiple bonds, they are chemically stable and relatively unreactive — except in combustion and halogenation reactions. General formula: CnH2n+2.

⬡ Alkenes & Alkynes — Unsaturated

Unsaturated hydrocarbons contain double or triple C–C bonds. These π-electron-rich regions are highly reactive, readily undergoing addition reactions with H₂, Br₂, HX, and H₂O — critical in industrial syntheses.

⬡ The n=1 Exception

Both alkenes and alkynes cannot exist with a single carbon. A double or triple bond requires at least two carbon atoms. Ethene (C₂H₄) and Ethyne (C₂H₂) are the minimum members of their respective series.

// Naming Science

IUPAC Nomenclature

The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry rules ensure every molecule has one unique, unambiguous systematic name. Follow these five steps in order.

Find the Parent Chain

Identify the longest continuous chain of carbon atoms. This determines the root name: Meth- (1C), Eth- (2C), Prop- (3C), But- (4C), Pent- (5C), Hex- (6C), Hept- (7C), Oct- (8C), Non- (9C), Dec- (10C).

Key rule for unsaturated compounds: The parent chain must include the double or triple bond — even if a longer chain exists that excludes it.

ExampleCH₃–CH₂–CH=CH₂ → Parent chain = 4 carbons → "But-"

The Lowest Locant Rule

Number the chain from the end that gives the lowest possible locant to the first point of difference — multiple bonds have priority over simple alkyl substituents.

  • Start numbering from the end closest to the double/triple bond.
  • If no multiple bonds: number from the end nearest the first branch.
  • If tied: use alphabetical order to break the tie.

Name the Substituents

Branches not part of the parent chain are named by replacing "-ane" with "-yl":

  • –CH₃ → Methyl
  • –C₂H₅ → Ethyl
  • –C₃H₇ → Propyl

Use prefixes di-, tri-, tetra- for identical groups: e.g., 2,2-dimethyl...

Alphabetical Priority

When multiple different substituents are present, list them in strict alphabetical order — based on the substituent name itself, ignoring multiplicative prefixes (di, tri, etc.).

Correct orderEthyl comes before Methyl → "...ethyl...methyl..."

Final Construction

Assemble the full name as: [locant]-[substituent]-[parent][suffix]

  • Use commas between numbers: 2,3-
  • Use hyphens between numbers and words: 2-methyl
  • No space before the parent chain name.
Full worked example2,2-dimethylpentane  ·  3-ethyl-2-methylhexane
// Structure Logic

Isomerism: Same Formula, Different Structure

Isomers share the same molecular formula but differ in structural arrangement or spatial orientation — often resulting in dramatically different physical and chemical properties. Two molecules can have the same atoms and still be completely different substances.

Type I

Structural (Constitutional) Isomers

Atoms bonded in a different order or connectivity. Three sub-types:

  • Chain isomers: Different skeleton branching. e.g. n-Butane (straight chain) vs Isobutane (branched) — both C₄H₁₀, but n-Butane boils at −1°C while Isobutane boils at −12°C.
  • Position isomers: Same functional group, different location on the chain. e.g. 1-Butene (C=C at position 1) vs 2-Butene (C=C at position 2), both C₄H₈.
  • Functional group isomers: Completely different functional groups. e.g. Ethanol (C₂H₅OH, an alcohol) vs Dimethyl Ether (CH₃OCH₃, an ether) — same formula C₂H₆O, totally different reactivity.
Type II

Stereoisomers (3D Arrangement)

Same bonding connectivity, but different spatial arrangement of atoms in 3D space. These can only be detected when you consider the geometry of the molecule:

  • Geometric (Cis/Trans): Arises from restricted rotation around a C=C double bond. Cis = same groups on the same side. Trans = same groups on opposite sides. e.g. Cis-2-Butene and Trans-2-Butene have different boiling points despite being C₄H₈.
  • Optical (Enantiomers): Non-superimposable mirror images — like your left and right hands. Arise from a chiral carbon (a carbon bonded to 4 different groups). Critical in pharmaceuticals — one enantiomer may be medicinal while the other is harmful.

💡 Why Does Isomerism Matter?

Isomerism is one of the main reasons organic chemistry has millions of known compounds. A single molecular formula like C₅H₁₂ corresponds to 3 different structural isomers (pentane, isopentane, neopentane), each with unique boiling points, solubility, and reactivity. In pharmaceuticals, getting the wrong isomer can mean the difference between a medicine and a poison — which is why understanding this concept is critical in real-world chemistry.

// Counting Matter

The Mole Concept

The mole is chemistry's counting unit — just like a "dozen" means 12, a "mole" means 6.02 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number). It bridges the atomic world and the measurable world: atoms are too small to count one by one, so we group them into moles we can weigh on a scale. These triangle formulas show the key relationships — cover the unknown quantity to reveal the formula to use.

💡 Real-World Analogy

If you had 1 mole of rice grains, they would cover the entire surface of Earth to a depth of about 75 metres. That's how large Avogadro's number (6.02 × 10²³) actually is — yet a single mole of carbon is just 12 grams. This is why the mole concept is so powerful: it lets us count trillions of invisible atoms using nothing but a lab scale.

// Energy & Heat

Thermochemistry

Thermochemistry studies the heat changes that accompany chemical reactions and physical processes. Every chemical reaction either releases or absorbs energy — understanding this is essential for predicting whether a reaction is spontaneous, designing fuels, and interpreting energy diagrams.

Exothermic Reactions

Release heat to the surroundings. The system loses energy, so ΔH is negative. Products are at lower energy than reactants. Examples: combustion of methane, neutralization of acid and base, rusting of iron.

Endothermic Reactions

Absorb heat from the surroundings. The system gains energy, so ΔH is positive. Products are at higher energy than reactants. Examples: photosynthesis, dissolving ammonium nitrate in water, thermal decomposition.

Enthalpy (ΔH)

Enthalpy is the total heat content of a system at constant pressure. We can't measure absolute enthalpy — only the change (ΔH = Hproducts − Hreactants). Standard enthalpy values are measured at 25°C and 1 atm.

// Nuclear Physics

Nuclear Chemistry

Nuclear chemistry deals with the structure of atomic nuclei and the transformations they undergo. Unlike ordinary chemical reactions which involve electrons only, nuclear reactions change the nucleus itself — transforming one element into another, releasing enormous energy described by Einstein's E = mc². It underpins nuclear energy, medical imaging, cancer treatment, and radiocarbon dating.

Half-Life Time

H.L.P = Total Time ÷ No. of Periods
Step 1 — Know
Identify: Initial Mass, Remaining Mass, Total Time, and Half-Life Period (H.L.P).
Step 2 — Find Periods
No. of Periods = Total Time ÷ H.L.P. Each period halves the remaining mass.
Step 3 — Remaining Mass
Remaining = Initial Mass ÷ 2ⁿ, where n is the number of half-life periods elapsed.
Example
100g sample, H.L.P = 5 years, Total Time = 20 years → 4 periods → Remaining = 100 ÷ 16 = 6.25g

// Bond Theory

Chemical Bonding

Chemical bonds are the forces holding atoms together. The type of bond depends on electronegativity differences between the atoms involved.

⊕⊖
Ionic Bond

Transfer of electrons from metal to non-metal. ΔEN > 1.7.

  • Forms crystal lattice structures
  • High melting/boiling points
  • Conducts electricity when dissolved
  • e.g. NaCl, MgO, CaCl₂
⬡⬡
Covalent Bond

Sharing of electrons between non-metals. ΔEN < 1.7.

  • Single, double, or triple bonds
  • Lower melting/boiling points
  • Poor conductors generally
  • e.g. H₂O, CO₂, CH₄, N₂
⚙️
Metallic Bond

Sea of delocalized electrons around positive metal ion lattice.

  • Excellent electrical conductors
  • Malleable and ductile
  • High melting points generally
  • e.g. Cu, Fe, Al, Au

Electronegativity Rules

  • ΔEN = 0 → Non-polar covalent (H₂, O₂)
  • ΔEN 0.1–1.7 → Polar covalent (H₂O, HCl)
  • ΔEN > 1.7 → Ionic bond (NaCl)

Bond Polarity

Electrons spend more time near the more electronegative atom, creating δ+ and δ− partial charges. This gives molecules like H₂O their unique properties.

Lewis Dot Structures

Dots = valence electrons. Pairs between atoms = bonds. Octet rule: atoms seek 8 valence electrons (H seeks 2). Lone pairs don't bond directly.

// Chemical Reactions

Types of Reactions

Chemical reactions are classified by how reactants transform into products. Recognizing the type helps predict products and balance equations.

Type 01

Synthesis (Combination)

Two or more substances combine into one product.

A + B → AB
2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
Type 02

Decomposition

One compound breaks into two or more simpler substances.

AB → A + B
2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂
Type 03

Single Displacement

One element replaces another in a compound. Activity series determines feasibility.

A + BC → AC + B
Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑
Type 04

Double Displacement

Two compounds exchange partners — often yields precipitate or water.

AB + CD → AD + CB
NaCl + AgNO₃ → AgCl↓ + NaNO₃
Type 05

Combustion

Substance reacts with O₂ releasing energy. Hydrocarbons → CO₂ + H₂O.

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O + energy
Type 06

Neutralization

Acid + Base → Salt + Water. H⁺ and OH⁻ combine to form H₂O.

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
// pH Chemistry

Acids & Bases

Acids donate protons (H⁺); bases accept protons or donate OH⁻. The pH scale measures acidity on a 0–14 logarithmic scale.

◀ Acidic (pH < 7) ◆ Neutral (pH = 7) Basic (pH > 7) ▶
Acids

Donate H⁺. pH < 7. Taste sour.

  • Strong: HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃, HBr, HI
  • Weak: CH₃COOH, H₂CO₃, H₃PO₄
  • pH = −log[H⁺]
  • Turn blue litmus red
Bases

Accept H⁺ or donate OH⁻. pH > 7. Feel slippery.

  • Strong: NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂, Ba(OH)₂
  • Weak: NH₃, Mg(OH)₂
  • pOH = −log[OH⁻]   pH + pOH = 14
  • Turn red litmus blue

Neutralization

H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O. The resulting salt may be acidic, basic, or neutral depending on relative strengths of the original acid and base.

Indicators

  • Litmus: red in acid, blue in base
  • Phenolphthalein: colourless → pink at pH 8.3
  • Methyl orange: red → yellow at pH 4.4
  • Universal: full colour pH spectrum

Buffers

Resist pH change when small acid/base amounts are added. Weak acid + conjugate base. Blood uses HCO₃⁻/H₂CO₃ to maintain pH ≈ 7.4.

// Redox & Cells

Electrochemistry

Electrochemistry studies the relationship between chemical reactions and electrical energy, including oxidation-reduction and electrochemical cells.

OIL
Oxidation Is Loss of electrons
|
RIG
Reduction Is Gain of electrons

Half Reactions

Oxidation (Anode −)

Loses electrons. Oxidation number increases.

Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻

Reduction (Cathode +)

Gains electrons. Oxidation number decreases.

Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu

Galvanic Cell

Chemical energy → electricity spontaneously (ΔG < 0). Oxidation at anode, reduction at cathode. Salt bridge maintains neutrality. e.g. Zn-Cu cell.

Electrolytic Cell

Uses electricity to drive non-spontaneous reactions (ΔG > 0). Used in electroplating and refining metals. External power source drives current.

Oxidation Number Rules

  • Pure elements = 0
  • Monoatomic ions = their charge
  • O = −2 (peroxides = −1)
  • H = +1 (hydrides = −1)
  • Neutral compound sum = 0

⚡ Real-World Applications

Electrochemistry is everywhere. Galvanic cells power every battery in your phone, car, and laptop. Electrolytic cells are used to purify copper (99.99% purity for electrical wiring), electroplate jewellery with gold or silver, and produce chlorine and aluminium industrially. Corrosion — the rusting of iron — is also an electrochemical process where iron is oxidised by oxygen in the presence of water.

// Gas Behavior

The Gas Laws

Gas laws describe mathematical relationships between pressure (P), volume (V), temperature (T) and moles (n) for ideal gases.

Boyle's Law

P & V

P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

At constant T: P and V are inversely proportional.

  • T and n constant
  • P↑ → V↓
Charles's Law

V & T

V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂

At constant P: V and T are directly proportional.

  • T in Kelvin always
Gay-Lussac's Law

P & T

P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂

At constant V: P and T are directly proportional.

  • T in Kelvin always
Combined Gas Law

P, V & T

P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂

Merges Boyle's, Charles's and Gay-Lussac's. n constant.

Ideal Gas Law

PV = nRT

PV = nRT

R = 8.314 J/(mol·K). n = moles. Assumes ideal behaviour.

Avogadro's Law

V & n

V₁/n₁ = V₂/n₂

Equal V at same T, P → equal moles. 1 mol = 22.4 L at STP.
