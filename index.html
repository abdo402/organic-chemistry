What are Hydrocarbons?

Hydrocarbons are the simplest organic compounds. They consist entirely of Carbon (C) and Hydrogen (H). They are the building blocks of fossil fuels, plastics, and complex organic molecules.

1. The Saturated Group (Alkanes)

Alkanes contain the maximum number of hydrogen atoms for each carbon. They are called "saturated" because there are no double or triple bonds to "add" more hydrogens. They are generally unreactive except in combustion.

2. The Unsaturated Group (Alkenes/Alkynes)

Unsaturated hydrocarbons have "room" for more hydrogen atoms because they contain double or triple bonds. These bonds are electron-rich and make these molecules very reactive in chemical syntheses.

3. The "n=1" Exception

Alkenes and Alkynes cannot have only 1 carbon atom. Why? Because a double or triple bond must exist between TWO carbon atoms. Therefore, the series starts from Ethene (n=2) and Ethyne (n=2).

Naming Science

IUPAC Nomenclature Masterclass

The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) developed these rules to ensure every molecule has a unique, systematic name.

Find the Parent Chain

Search for the longest continuous chain of carbon atoms. This chain determines the base name (Meth-, Eth-, Prop-, etc.).

Important: If an alkene/alkyne is present, the parent chain MUST include the double/triple bond, even if a longer chain without it exists.

The Lowest Locant Rule

Number the carbon atoms in the parent chain starting from the end that gives the lowest possible number to the multiple bond or the substituent (branch).

Multiple bonds take priority over alkyl branches in numbering.

Name the Branches

Any carbon groups not part of the parent chain are "Substituents". We name them by adding the suffix "-yl".

  • 1 Carbon branch = Methyl
  • 2 Carbon branch = Ethyl
  • 3 Carbon branch = Propyl

Alphabetical Priority

When multiple branches exist, list them in alphabetical order in the final name (Ethyl before Methyl), regardless of their position number on the chain.

Final Construction

Combine everything: [Locant]-[Branch][Parent][Suffix].

Use commas to separate numbers (2,2-) and hyphens to separate numbers from letters (2-methyl).

Example: 2,2-dimethylpentane.

Structure Logic

Isomerism: Same Formula, Different Soul

Isomerism is the existence of molecules that have the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements.

1. Structural (Constitutional) Isomers

Atoms are linked in different orders. This includes:

  • Chain Isomers: Different branching of the carbon skeleton (e.g., Butane vs Isobutane).
  • Position Isomers: Same group, different location (e.g., 1-Butene vs 2-Butene).
  • Functional Isomers: Different functional groups (e.g., Ethanol vs Dimethyl Ether).

2. Stereoisomers (3D Space)

Same bonding order, but different 3D arrangement in space.

  • Geometrical (Cis/Trans): Occurs due to restricted rotation around double bonds.
  • Optical (Enantiomers): Mirror images that are not superimposable (Chirality).
